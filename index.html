Hi! I'm Kiran Mak

Curious, Driven, Impact Oriented

I hope to make a difference in the world

Every day I look to learn and challenge myself

For me, having a purpose in life means doing something that actually matters, it means that I want my work to have a tangible impact on the environment and people around me. Of all of the many problems facing us now, I care about climate change and improving environmental sustainability and I want to fight for animal rights. Further, I hope to change our education system to give all children access to high quality and personalized education.

In keeping with the idea that knowedge is the path forwards, my favorite times are when I can get lost in a book or down a rabbit hole of research. I want to know how things work and why so I've enjoyed about science topics from astronomy to immunology. And as a wrap up to the evergrowing list of interesting things I'll add computer science. I've experimented with programming, iOS development, and ML algorithms over the past six years and it really is crazy what we can do. It's amazing that computers can find patterns where to us it's just randomness.

This past year I've explored my interest in computational materials science as an Engineering Intern at META, a metamaterials company. There I've worked on projects optimizing designs for solar cell (for maximal transmission) and transparent conductive webs. I've rerealized how little I know, and learned the importance of finding the right questions in science.

My interest in materials science began because it was a facsinating combination of physics, engineering, chemistry, biology, and computation with a near infinite number of practical applications. One area I find particularly interesting is programmable materials, materials that can move and react on their own to an external stiumulus (without motors or sensors) solely because of their physiochemical structure. It enables the creation of simple, dynamic, and customizable objects like blinds that automatically open to let in sunshine, clothing that molds to you based on your body heat, or furniture that self assembles out of the package. Second is the emergence of computational materials science. Some of the largest challenges with materials are designing them and modeling how they will behave so we're stuck with trial and error which is time and resource intensive. Using computational tools we'll be able to design a material optimized for a specific task, understand the structure-function relationships, and model for example a soft robot.
